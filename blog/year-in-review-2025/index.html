Building in Public: Our 2025 Year in Review

By Kive AppsDecember 30, 20257 min read

TL;DR

  • 2025 was our foundational year as an indie studio.
  • We built the core encryption engine and API infrastructure.
  • We learned that shipping offline-first is much harder than it looks.

It's easy to write the polished version of a year-in-review post: clean numbers, neat lessons, a wholesome conclusion. The honest version is messier. Here's the real one.

What we shipped

  • The Kive Apps brand. We started the year unsure whether we even wanted a brand. By December the answer was a clear yes — a brand makes it easier to build adjacent products without restarting trust every time.
  • The marketing site. Three iterations. The first was over-engineered. The second was too plain. The third (this one) is what we kept.
  • LifeMaster AI v0 internal builds. The first internal alpha was unusable. v0.4 became something we'd actually use. v0.9 went to a private beta. v1.0 ships in early 2026.
  • The encrypted-config system. Our biggest engineering investment of the year. It lets us push remote configuration to apps without ever sending plaintext over the wire. We'll write a separate post on it.

What worked

Saying no a lot

The single most useful habit was rejecting features that didn't survive the question "would I use this every week?" That filter killed half the roadmap. The remaining half is the app.

Writing before coding

For every feature, we wrote the in-app description, the tutorial copy, and the marketing line before writing any code. If we couldn't make the description compelling, we didn't build the feature. This pre-mortem caught maybe a dozen ideas that would have otherwise consumed weeks.

Privacy as a constraint

Treating "no analytics, no cloud, no tracking" as a hard requirement made design decisions easier, not harder. When the architecture options narrow, you stop spending time choosing between bad options.

What didn't work

The first design system

We spent weeks on a custom design system that turned out to be over-fitted to a single screen. We threw it out and rebuilt with simpler primitives. The lesson: design systems should emerge from screens, not precede them.

Trying to launch before ready

We had three "we're launching!" moments in 2025 that didn't result in a launch. Each time, we found a real reason to delay. By the third time we'd learned: self-imposed launch dates are useful as a forcing function but useless as a commitment. Ship when it's actually ready.

Newsletter

We tried building a newsletter. We didn't have anything to say weekly that was worth reading. We've put the newsletter on hold until we have actual things to share.

Numbers we tracked (and the ones we didn't)

The vanity metrics nobody asked for:

  • Lines of code written and deleted: roughly 60k each. Net codebase size went up about 15k. Most of the work was simplification.
  • Internal beta users: 23. Most of them are friends and family.
  • Cups of chai consumed: untracked but substantial.

What we deliberately didn't track:

  • Daily/weekly active users (we don't have analytics)
  • Engagement metrics (same)
  • Revenue (none yet)

We don't think these are bad metrics in general. We just don't think they're useful for us right now. The thing that matters in year one of a small studio is whether the founders still believe in the product. That's a yes.

The unexpected lesson

Here's the thing nobody tells you about indie app building: the loneliness is real, but it's also the secret weapon. Without a team to consult, you make decisions faster. Without investors, you can take a year to ship something. Without a marketing budget, you have to build something good enough that people tell each other about it.

The constraints that look like disadvantages are also the constraints that produce a different kind of product.

Constraints are the medium. The work emerges from them.

What 2026 looks like

  • Q1: Public launch of LifeMaster AI v1.0.
  • Q2: Localization (Hindi first), tablet layout, voice journaling.
  • Q3: Begin work on app #2. We have a shortlist; we won't share it until we're sure.
  • Q4: Honest assessment of whether we should keep going or do something else with our time.

We'll write the 2026 review at the end of next year. If you want to follow along, the easiest way is to grab the app, send us a real reply when something resonates, and tell one friend.

Thanks to everyone who emailed, tested, and gave feedback in 2025. We didn't reply to all of you. We read all of you.
